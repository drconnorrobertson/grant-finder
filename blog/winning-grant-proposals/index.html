10 Tips for Writing a Winning Grant Proposal

By Dr. Connor Robertson, Founder of GrantFinder · February 14, 2026 · 7 min read

Writing a compelling grant proposal is one of the most important skills a nonprofit can develop. After years of working with organizations seeking funding, I have seen certain patterns emerge that separate successful proposals from the rest. Here are ten strategies that consistently produce results.

  1. Start with the funder, not yourself. Before writing a single word, study the funder's mission, past awards, and stated priorities. Your proposal should demonstrate alignment between their goals and your work. Funders want to invest in organizations that share their vision, not just organizations that need money.
  2. Lead with impact, not need. While it is tempting to lead with the problem you are solving, the most compelling proposals open with the impact your organization will create. Funders want to see what their dollars will accomplish. Frame your narrative around outcomes and change, not just deficits and challenges.
  3. Tell a specific story. Abstract statements about community need are less persuasive than concrete stories. Instead of saying "many families lack access to healthy food," describe a real scenario: the single parent working two jobs who relies on your mobile food pantry to feed her children. Specificity creates emotional connection.
  4. Use data strategically. Combine your narrative with strong data points. Cite local statistics, program outcomes from previous grants, and relevant research. Data lends credibility to your story and shows funders that you understand the landscape you operate in.
  5. Build a realistic budget. A well-constructed budget demonstrates competence. Every line item should be justifiable and directly connected to the proposed activities. Include a clear budget narrative that explains your assumptions. Funders notice when budgets do not add up or when costs seem inflated.
  6. Define measurable outcomes. Go beyond vague goals like "improve community health." Define specific, measurable outcomes: "provide nutrition education to 200 families and reduce food insecurity by 30% among participants within 12 months." SMART goals show funders you are serious about accountability.
  7. Demonstrate organizational capacity. Funders need confidence that your organization can execute the proposed program. Highlight relevant experience, staff qualifications, existing partnerships, and past successes. If you are a newer organization, emphasize the expertise your team brings from other roles.
  8. Address sustainability. Most funders want to know what happens after their grant period ends. Outline a clear plan for sustaining the program, whether through diversified funding, earned revenue, or integration into existing operations. This signals long-term thinking and responsible stewardship.
  9. Follow instructions precisely. This may seem obvious, but a surprising number of proposals are rejected for failing to meet basic submission requirements. Follow word counts, formatting guidelines, and required attachments exactly. If the funder asks for three references, provide three, not two and not five.
  10. Get outside feedback before submitting. Have someone unfamiliar with your program read the proposal. If they cannot clearly explain what you do, whom you serve, and what outcomes you expect, the proposal needs revision. Fresh eyes catch assumptions, jargon, and gaps that insiders miss.

Putting It All Together

Grant writing is both an art and a science. The art is in storytelling and persuasion. The science is in data, budgets, and evaluation design. The best proposals weave these elements together seamlessly.
